SolarTrack: An Efficient Radiometric Model for Wearable Solar Harvesting

Abstract. This article presents an efficient radiometric model for continuous tracking of hand motion using wearable solar cells. Our approach leverages the geometric relationship between hand position and a light source to enable power-based tracking without occlusion. The model provides a foundation for gesture recognition, activity monitoring, and other motion-sensing applications in wearable devices.

Introduction

Wearable solar cells offer a unique opportunity for continuous sensing by harvesting ambient energy while simultaneously providing information about device motion and orientation. Traditional motion tracking relies on inertial measurement units (IMUs) or cameras, which consume significant power. In contrast, solar-based tracking leverages the energy harvesting process itself, making it an attractive solution for battery-constrained wearable devices[1].

Our radiometric model captures the relationship between hand motion and harvested power under controlled lighting conditions. By modeling the geometric configuration of the solar cell relative to the light source, we can infer continuous hand position and orientation. This approach enables applications ranging from gesture-based interfaces to activity monitoring without additional sensing hardware.

Model Formulation

The harvested power \(P\) from a wearable solar cell can be modeled as a function of geometric parameters and cell properties:

\[P = \kappa \cdot \mathcal{F}(a, H) + \epsilon\]
(1)

where:

  • \(\kappa\) is a scaling factor capturing cell efficiency, area, and light intensity
  • \(\mathcal{F}(a, H)\) is the view factor between the cell and light source
  • \(a\) is the lateral offset (horizontal distance) in millimeters
  • \(H\) is the vertical separation (height) in millimeters
  • \(\epsilon\) represents measurement noise

View Factor Computation

The view factor \(\mathcal{F}(a, H)\) quantifies the fraction of radiation leaving the light source that reaches the solar cell. For a circular disk (solar cell) and a circular light source, we compute this using numerical integration:

\[\mathcal{F}(a, H) = \frac{1}{\pi r^2} \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^r \frac{\cos\theta_1 \cos\theta_2}{\pi L^2} \, r' \, dr' \, d\phi\]
(2)

where \(r\) is the cell radius, \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\) are the angles between surface normals and the line connecting the surfaces, and \(L\) is the distance between differential surface elements.

Orientation-Aware Extension

For applications requiring orientation tracking, we extend the model to account for cell tilt using Lambert's cosine law:

\[\mathcal{F}_{\text{oriented}}(a, H, \theta) = \mathcal{F}(a, H) \cdot \max(0, \cos\theta)\]
(3)

where \(\theta\) is the incidence angle between the cell normal and the light direction vector. This extension enables continuous orientation tracking in addition to position[2].

As the hand moves through 3D space, the changing values of \(a\) and \(H\) create a unique power signature that can be used for tracking. The relationship is smooth and deterministic under the assumption of no occlusion, making it suitable for continuous motion inference.

Power Level Sets

The level sets in Figure 2 reveal the structure of the power function. Points along a single curve produce the same harvested power, representing the ambiguity inherent in single-source tracking. By observing temporal sequences of power measurements, we can disambiguate position along these curves and perform continuous tracking[3].

Applications

Continuous Hand Tracking

The primary application of our model is continuous hand tracking for wearable devices. By mounting a solar cell on the wrist or hand, we can track 3D motion trajectories without additional sensors. This enables always-on tracking with zero power overhead, as the tracking information is obtained during the energy harvesting process.

Gesture Recognition

Temporal sequences of power measurements contain rich information about hand gestures. Our model provides the foundation for recognizing gestures such as circles, swipes, and other patterns. Combined with machine learning classifiers, this approach can enable gesture-based interfaces for smartwatches and other wearable devices.

Activity Monitoring

Beyond explicit gestures, our model can be used for continuous activity monitoring. Different activities produce characteristic power patterns that can be used to infer user behavior, providing valuable context for health and fitness applications.

Limitations and Future Work

Our current model assumes no occlusion between the solar cell and light source. In practice, self-occlusion (e.g., from fingers) and environmental occlusion can affect the power signal. Future work will address these challenges through robust estimation techniques and multi-sensor fusion.

Additionally, the model currently assumes a single stationary light source. Extending to multiple or moving light sources would broaden the applicability to real-world scenarios with complex lighting conditions.

Conclusion

We have presented an efficient radiometric model for continuous hand tracking using wearable solar cells. The model provides a principled approach to inferring 3D position and orientation from harvested power measurements. By leveraging the energy harvesting process itself, our approach enables zero-power motion sensing for battery-constrained wearable devices.

The interactive demo accompanying this article allows readers to explore the model's behavior under different conditions and gain intuition about the geometric relationships governing solar-based tracking.
